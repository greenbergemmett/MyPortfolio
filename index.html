Projects

Analysis of Building Permit Valuations in Boston

This project uses R to determine the factors most correlated with building permit values in Boston, particularly the differences between commercial and residential permit values. Skills essential to this analysis include aggregation, merging data sets, making maps, and statistical inference using t-tests and multivariate regression.

My analysis found that characteristics of the surrounding population such as income, race/ethnicity, and transportation access tend to be associated with permit activities and expenditures. Addressing the inequities in places with lower permit values is critical to improving the affordability of housing and residential properties, as well as improving the affordability and access to goods and services that commercial properties provide. The skills of analyzing this data set, especially multivariate regression, can be applied more broadly to addressing the housing and rental affordability crisis in Boston.

Mapping Pedestrian Safety in Environmental Justice Communities in Boston

This project uses ArcGIS Pro to explore pedestrian safety in Environmental Justice (EJ) communities in Boston. In my analysis, I aimed to find correlations between pedestrian crashes, sidewalk quality, number of reported safety concerns, and EJ criteria such median income, minority population, and English language proficiency. I used Spatial Joins on each layer with the EJ block groups layer to summarize the totals for each block group. The maps I created suggest a moderate negative correlation between crashes and the quality of pedestrian infrastructure. However, the pedestrian crash counts do not appear to be correlated with the low-income block groups any more than they are correlated with block groups that are not low-income.

Based on my results, I would recommend that the city try to improve the condition of its sidewalks in Environmental Justice communities. This might mean repairing the concrete, widening sidewalks where feasible, or adding dedicated pedestrian facilities such as footpaths, bridges, and ramps. To address safety concerns, the city could implement traffic calming measures, such as speed humps and pedestrian refuge islands. Other recommendations include talking to people in the community about their needs, conducting traffic studies, and observing driver and pedestrian behavior in these communities.

Visualizing Traffic Patterns in Massachusetts

This project uses R to analyze changes in traffic patterns in Massachusetts from 2019 to 2020, during the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. I created visualizations showing changes in speed and the number of car accidents by road type (interstate, arterial, local road, etc.), using boxplots to show the distributions of each of these two variables. Using Canva, I created an infograhpic which spotlights the communities in Massachusetts that saw the biggest increase in accidents in 2020. It also displays trends occurring on different types of roads as well as correlations between speed and accidents.

My findings found that while most road types saw fewer accidents in 2020 compared to 2019, local roads averaged about two more accidents in 2020, in addition to average higher speeds of 4.68 mph.
